Nothing derails a notary appointment faster than reaching for your wallet and realizing your ID isn't going to cut it. Let's make sure that doesn't happen to you — here's exactly what counts as acceptable identification for a Florida notarization.

## The Core Requirement

Florida notary law requires "satisfactory evidence" of identity, which in practice means a current, government-issued photo ID. The notary's whole job hinges on confirming you are who you claim to be, so this step isn't something that can be skipped or worked around casually.

## IDs That Are Generally Accepted

- **Florida driver's license** (or another state's)
- **State-issued ID card**
- **U.S. passport or passport card**
- **U.S. military ID**
- **Permanent resident card (green card)**
- **Foreign passport**, in many cases, depending on the notary's comfort verifying it

The common thread: government-issued, includes a photo, and is current.

## What About Expired IDs?

This is where people get caught off guard. Florida law allows identification documents issued within the past five years, even if technically expired, as long as they contain a photo and signature and reasonably resemble the person presenting them. In plain terms: a license that expired eight months ago is often still fine. One that expired three years ago generally isn't.

If your only ID is expired, it's worth checking with the notary beforehand rather than assuming either way — some notaries apply this more conservatively than others, and it's better to know before the appointment than to find out at the door.

## What If You Don't Have Acceptable ID at All?

Florida law does provide a backup option: identification through the sworn statement of a "credible witness" — someone who personally knows both the notary and the person signing, and is willing to vouch for their identity under oath. This route has specific requirements and isn't always practical (finding someone the notary *also* personally knows isn't simple), but it exists for genuine situations where standard ID isn't available.

Realistically, the far easier path is simply locating a current, valid photo ID before your appointment. If there's any doubt about whether you have something acceptable, a quick text photo of your ID to confirm ahead of time can save a wasted trip.

## A Few Practical Tips

- **Check your ID's expiration date before booking**, not the day of.
- **Bring the physical document**, not a photo of it on your phone — Florida notarizations require the original.
- **If your legal name has changed** (marriage, divorce, etc.) and your ID hasn't caught up, mention this beforehand so the notary can plan for it.
- **For [Remote Online Notary](/remote-online-notary) sessions**, you'll typically go through an ID scan as part of the platform's identity verification process — have the same acceptable ID ready for that.

## Why Notaries Are Strict About This

It might feel like a small technicality when you're standing there with an ID that's "basically valid," but this is genuinely the entire point of notarization. A notary's signature and seal are a legal statement that they personally verified who signed a document. Being strict about ID isn't bureaucratic stubbornness — it's what makes a notarized document trustworthy in the first place.
