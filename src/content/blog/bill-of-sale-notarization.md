A bill of sale sounds like a formality — proof you bought or sold something — but whether it needs a notary's involvement depends on what's being sold and where it's headed. Here's the breakdown.

## What a Bill of Sale Actually Documents

A bill of sale is a simple record of a transaction: what was sold, for how much, between which parties, and when. It's most commonly used for private sales of vehicles, boats, and other significant personal property where there isn't already a formal transfer document handling the same job.

## When Notarization Is Actually Required

Requirements vary by what's being sold:

- **Vehicles** — Florida doesn't universally require a notarized bill of sale for private car sales, though some situations (older titles, specific circumstances) may call for it
- **Vessels (boats)** — some vessel title transfers and registrations require a notarized bill of sale, particularly for larger or titled boats; check current FLHSMV requirements for your specific vessel
- **High-value personal property** — not typically required, but sometimes requested by one party for extra assurance

The safest approach for any specific transaction: check the current requirements for that exact item type, since rules can be specific to the category of property involved.

## Why Notarize One Even When It's Optional

Even when notarization isn't strictly required, it adds real value to a bill of sale:

- **Verified identity** of both parties involved
- **A timestamped, witnessed record** of the transaction
- **Extra credibility** if the sale is ever questioned or disputed later
- **Peace of mind for both buyer and seller**, particularly in private sales between people who don't know each other well

Given how quick and inexpensive notarizing a single-page document typically is, it's often worth doing even when not legally mandated.

## Bill of Sale vs. Title: Not the Same Thing

This distinction trips people up. A bill of sale documents the *transaction* — the sale itself. A title (for a vehicle or titled vessel) is the official *ownership* document. You may need both for a complete, properly documented sale: the bill of sale as a record of what happened, and the title transfer as the official change of ownership.
