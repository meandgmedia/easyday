At some point, almost everyone ends up staring at a form that says "must be notarized" and wondering, quietly, *why*. It's a fair question. Notarization exists to prevent fraud on documents where the stakes are high enough that a signature alone isn't considered sufficient proof of who signed it and why.

Rather than explain the theory all day, here's the practical version: 25 documents people notarize regularly, grouped by category, so you can quickly check whether yours is on the list.

## Real Estate Documents

Property transactions involve some of the highest-stakes paperwork most people ever sign, which is exactly why notarization shows up so often here.

1. **Warranty deeds** — transferring property with a guarantee of clear title
2. **Quit claim deeds** — transferring property without that guarantee, often used between family members
3. **Mortgage and refinance documents** — the bulk of a loan closing package
4. **HELOC agreements** — home equity line of credit paperwork
5. **Lease agreements** — not always required, but common for longer-term commercial leases

If you're in the middle of a closing, our [loan signings page](/loan-signings) has more on how that process works.

## Estate Planning & Power of Attorney

These documents carry enough legal weight that notarization is either required or strongly recommended across the board.

6. **Durable power of attorney** — authorizing someone to act on your behalf financially
7. **Medical power of attorney** — authorizing healthcare decisions on your behalf
8. **Healthcare surrogate designations** — similar to a medical POA, naming who decides for you
9. **Living wills / advance directives** — outlining your medical wishes
10. **Trust documents** — establishing or amending a living trust
11. **Codicils** — amendments to an existing will

## Affidavits & Sworn Statements

Anything where you're swearing, under oath, that something is true generally needs a notary present.

12. **General affidavits** — sworn statements used for a wide range of purposes
13. **Affidavits of identity** — confirming you are who your documents say you are
14. **Name change affidavits**
15. **Affidavits of residency**
16. **Small estate affidavits** — used in simplified probate situations

## Business & Financial Documents

17. **LLC operating agreements**
18. **Corporate resolutions**
19. **Business contracts** requiring notarization by policy or by one party's request
20. **Loan agreements** between private parties
21. **Franchise agreements**

## Personal & Family Documents

22. **Travel consent forms** — authorizing a minor to travel without one or both parents
23. **Adoption paperwork** — varies by stage of the process
24. **Divorce settlement agreements** — depending on jurisdiction and filing requirements

## Vehicle & DMV Documents

25. **Vehicle title transfers** — required in certain situations, particularly for older titles without a printed notary line, or out-of-state transfers

## A Quick Way to Check Your Own Document

If you're holding a form right now and wondering whether it needs notarizing, look for a few tells:

- A signature block labeled **"acknowledgment"** or **"jurat"**
- Language like **"sworn before me"** or **"subscribed and sworn"**
- Instructions from the receiving agency explicitly stating notarization is required

When in doubt, the safest move is asking the institution requesting the document — or just [contacting us](/contact) with a photo of the signature page, and we'll take a look.

## What a Notary Can't Do

Worth mentioning, since it comes up: a notary can confirm you're who you say you are and witness your signature, but they can't tell you whether the document itself is legally sound, draft it for you, or give advice about its contents. That's an attorney's job. A notary's role is narrower — and honestly, that narrowness is what makes the whole system trustworthy.
