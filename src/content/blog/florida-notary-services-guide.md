If you've never needed a document notarized before, the process can feel
more mysterious than it should. In Florida, notarization is a well-defined,
regulated act — but knowing what to expect ahead of time makes the whole
experience faster and less stressful. Here's what you actually need to know.

## What a Notary Public Actually Does

A Florida Notary Public is a state-commissioned official who serves as an
impartial witness to the signing of important documents. The notary's job
is narrow but important: verify the identity of the signer, confirm the
signer appears to understand what they're signing and is doing so
willingly, witness the signature, and complete a notarial certificate that
makes the document legally recognizable as notarized.

A notary does not draft documents, does not provide legal advice, and does
not verify that the contents of a document are true or accurate. Their role
is to reduce fraud by confirming that the person who signed a document is
who they claim to be, and that they signed it knowingly.

## Common Documents That Require Notarization

Florida notaries are called on for a wide range of paperwork, including:

- Powers of attorney
- Real estate deeds and closing documents
- Affidavits and sworn statements
- Loan and mortgage documents
- Medical directives and healthcare surrogate forms
- Travel consent letters for minors
- Business agreements and corporate resolutions

Each of these has slightly different notarial requirements — some need an
"acknowledgment," others require a "jurat," which involves the signer
swearing to the truth of the document's contents under oath. Your notary
will determine the correct certificate type based on the document itself.

## What to Bring to Your Appointment

Preparation makes a notary appointment quick. You'll need:

1. **A valid, government-issued photo ID.** A driver's license, state ID,
   or passport are the most commonly accepted forms in Florida.
2. **Your unsigned document.** Florida law requires you to sign in the
   notary's presence — documents signed beforehand generally cannot be
   notarized.
3. **Any additional signers**, if the document requires more than one
   signature, since each signer typically needs to be present with their
   own ID.

## Mobile Notary vs. In-Office Notary

Traditionally, notarization meant visiting a bank, shipping store, or law
office during business hours. A mobile notary flips that model: instead of
you traveling to the notary, the notary travels to you — your home, office,
a hospital room, or anywhere else that works for your schedule. This is
especially useful for real estate closings, hospital signings, and anyone
juggling a full workday.

## Understanding Remote Online Notarization (RON)

Since 2020, Florida law has also permitted Remote Online Notarization,
which allows an entire notarization to happen over live video. The signer's
identity is verified electronically, the session is recorded, and documents
are signed using a secure electronic signature. RON has made it possible to
get documents notarized without leaving home at all, provided the receiving
party accepts electronically notarized documents.

## What Can Disqualify a Notarization

There are a few situations where a Florida notary is legally required to
decline a request:

- The signer cannot produce valid, satisfactory identification.
- The signer appears to be signing under duress, or does not seem to
  understand the document.
- The notary has a personal financial interest in the transaction.
- The document is already signed and the signer cannot re-sign in the
  notary's presence.

Knowing these ahead of time helps you avoid a wasted trip — or a wasted
mobile visit.

## How Much Does Notarization Cost in Florida?

Florida Statutes Chapter 117 sets maximum fees a notary may charge for the
notarial act itself (currently capped per signature/seal). Mobile notaries
may also charge a separate travel fee, and loan signing packages are often
priced based on the complexity of the closing. Always confirm pricing
before your appointment so there are no surprises.

## Choosing the Right Notary

Not every notary offers the same services. When choosing a Florida notary,
consider:

- Whether they offer mobile visits, remote online sessions, or both
- Their availability outside standard business hours
- Experience with the specific document type you need — loan signings, in
  particular, benefit from a notary who is trained as a Loan Signing Agent
- Clear, upfront communication about fees and scheduling
