Handing over a scan of your driver's license and sitting in front of a webcam to get something notarized can feel like it should come with more red flags than it does. It's a fair instinct — you're right to ask questions before trusting a process with something like a power of attorney. Here's an honest look at what's actually protecting you.

## The Short Answer

Yes, Remote Online Notarization is safe — genuinely, not just "safe enough." Florida built specific legal and technical requirements into RON precisely because lawmakers anticipated this exact question. The identity verification, recording, and tamper-evident signing built into the process are arguably more rigorous than what happens during a typical in-person notarization at a bank counter.

## What Actually Verifies Your Identity

A RON session doesn't just take your word for who you are. It layers multiple checks:

- **Government-issued ID scan** — the platform reads and validates your ID against known document formats
- **Knowledge-based authentication (KBA)** — a set of questions pulled from public and credit-header records, structured so that only you would reasonably know the answers
- **Live visual comparison** — the notary compares your face on camera to your ID photo in real time
- **Credential analysis** — the platform checks that the ID itself hasn't been tampered with or forged

That's considerably more verification than a bank teller glancing at your license for three seconds before stamping a form.

## Why the Recording Matters

Every RON session is recorded and retained, typically for years, as required by Florida law. This might feel intrusive at first, but it's actually a significant safety feature — it means there's a verifiable, timestamped record of exactly what happened: who appeared, what was reviewed, and that the signer appeared to understand and willingly sign the document. If a notarization is ever challenged later, that recording is the strongest possible evidence of what actually took place.

## Can a RON Document Be Faked or Altered?

This is where the technology genuinely shines. Electronic notarial seals and signatures use cryptographic methods that make undetected tampering extremely difficult — any change to the document after it's signed leaves a detectable trace. Compare that to a paper document, where a sufficiently skilled forger has historically had more room to work.

## Where the Real Risk Actually Lives

Ironically, the more realistic security concern isn't the RON process itself — it's the same thing that threatens any online activity: phishing, weak passwords, or signing on an unfamiliar public device. A few habits worth keeping:

- Only join your session through the link the notary or platform provides directly
- Use a private, secure internet connection rather than public Wi-Fi
- Keep your login credentials for the platform confidential, same as any other account

## Comparing to In-Person Notarization

## The Bottom Line

Online notarization isn't a looser, less trustworthy version of the traditional process — in several respects, it's a more rigorously verified one.
