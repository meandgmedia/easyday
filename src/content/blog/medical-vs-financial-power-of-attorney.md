"Power of attorney" gets used like it's one single thing, but it's actually shorthand for two fairly different documents that happen to share a name. Mixing them up is an easy mistake to make — and an important one to avoid, since they authorize completely different kinds of decisions.

## The Core Difference

**A financial (durable) power of attorney** authorizes someone to manage your money, property, and financial affairs — paying bills, managing bank accounts, handling investments, even selling property on your behalf if the document allows it.

**A medical (healthcare) power of attorney** authorizes someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you're unable to make them yourself — treatment choices, healthcare providers, and end-of-life decisions in line with your wishes.

One handles your wallet. The other handles your body. Two very different kinds of authority, which is exactly why Florida treats them as separate documents.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| | Financial POA | Medical POA |
|---|---|---|
| Also called | Durable power of attorney | Healthcare surrogate designation |
| Covers | Money, property, financial decisions | Medical treatment decisions |
| Takes effect | Often immediately (if "durable") | Typically when you're unable to communicate |
| Notarization required | Yes | Yes |
| Witnesses required | Two | Two |
| Governed by | Florida Statutes Chapter 709 | Florida Statutes Chapter 765 |

## Why "Durable" Matters for Financial POAs

You'll often see the word "durable" attached to a financial power of attorney, and it's not just legal decoration. A durable POA stays in effect even if you become incapacitated — which is usually the entire point of having one. A non-durable POA, by contrast, automatically ends if you become incapacitated, which somewhat defeats the purpose for most people's planning needs. If you're setting one up specifically as a safeguard, make sure "durable" language is actually in the document.

## Why Most People Eventually Need Both

It's easy to think you only need one or the other, but they cover such different territory that most complete estate plans include both. Consider a fairly common scenario: someone is hospitalized after a stroke. A medical POA lets a trusted person make treatment decisions. A financial POA lets that same person (or someone else) keep paying the mortgage, managing bank accounts, and handling bills while the person recovers. Without both, families are often stuck navigating two separate problems at the worst possible time.

## Choosing Who to Name

A few things worth thinking through:

- **Do you trust this person with your finances specifically?** Financial and medical judgment aren't always the same skill.
- **Are they local, or reasonably able to act quickly if needed?**
- **Have you talked to them about it?** Naming someone without a conversation can lead to confusion later.
- **Do you need a backup?** Naming a successor agent is smart in case your first choice is unavailable when it matters.
