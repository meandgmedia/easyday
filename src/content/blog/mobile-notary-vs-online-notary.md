Florida signers today have two convenient alternatives to the old model of
driving to a bank or office during business hours: mobile notary visits and
Remote Online Notarization (RON). Both get your document legally notarized
— but they work differently, and one may fit your situation better than the
other. Here's how to decide.

## The Short Answer

If your document requires an in-person witness by policy, if you're
uncomfortable with electronic signing, or if you have multiple physical
documents that need to stay in paper form, a **mobile notary** is usually
the better fit. If you value speed and convenience, don't have a Florida ID
accepted in person only, and your document's recipient accepts
electronically notarized files, **Remote Online Notarization** is often
faster and easier.

Let's break down both in more detail.

## What Is a Mobile Notary?

A mobile notary is a commissioned Florida Notary Public who travels to your
location — home, office, hospital, coffee shop, or wherever makes sense —
to perform the notarization in person. You sign a physical, paper document
in the notary's physical presence, and the notary applies their seal and
signature directly to that paper.

**Best for:**

- Real estate closings that require wet-ink signatures
- Signers who are hospitalized or homebound
- Situations where multiple signers need to sign the same physical document
  together
- Anyone who simply prefers an in-person appointment

**Trade-offs:**

- Requires coordinating a time and location that works for both parties
- May involve a travel fee depending on distance
- Limited to the notary's local service area (unless you're willing to pay
  for extended travel)

## What Is Remote Online Notarization (RON)?

Remote Online Notarization allows the entire notarization — identity
verification, witnessing, and signing — to happen over a live, recorded
video call. Florida has authorized RON since 2020, and it uses secure
identity verification methods (often including knowledge-based
authentication questions and ID document scanning) before the signer
electronically signs the document.

**Best for:**

- Signers anywhere in Florida, not just a notary's local area
- Time-sensitive documents that need same-day handling
- People with mobility limitations or unpredictable schedules
- Straightforward documents like affidavits, powers of attorney, or
  business agreements

**Trade-offs:**

- Requires a working camera, microphone, and stable internet connection
- Not every receiving institution accepts electronically notarized
  documents — it's worth confirming beforehand
- Some signers are simply more comfortable with an in-person interaction,
  especially for high-stakes documents

## A Side-by-Side Comparison

| | Mobile Notary | Remote Online Notary |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Notary travels to you | Anywhere with internet access |
| Format | Physical, paper document | Digital, electronically signed |
| Availability | Limited to local service area | Statewide |
| Typical speed | Same-day to next-day | Often same-day, sometimes same-hour |
| Best for | Closings, hospital visits, paper-only documents | Time-sensitive or straightforward documents |

## Questions to Ask Before You Choose

1. **Does the receiving party accept electronic notarization?** Some
   banks, courts, and agencies still require a wet-ink signature and
   physical seal. When in doubt, ask before scheduling.
2. **How comfortable are you with technology?** RON requires a device with
   a camera and a stable internet connection, plus the ability to complete
   an identity verification step online.
3. **How urgent is the document?** RON sessions can often be scheduled
   faster since there's no travel involved.
4. **Are multiple signers involved?** If everyone signing the document is
   in different locations, RON may actually be simpler than coordinating an
   in-person meeting.
