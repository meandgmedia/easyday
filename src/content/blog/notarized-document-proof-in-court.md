Notarization has a reputation for making a document "legally binding" or "official," which is true in a specific sense but easy to overstate. Here's a clearer picture of what notarization actually proves when a document ends up in a courtroom.

## What Notarization Actually Verifies

A notarization confirms two specific things: that the person who signed a document was verified to be who they claimed to be, and that they signed (or swore to the content, for a jurat) willingly, witnessed by the notary. That's the scope of what it proves.

## What Notarization Does NOT Prove

This is where the confusion usually happens. Notarization does **not** verify:

- **That the document's content is true or accurate** (except for sworn affidavits, where the signer themselves is swearing to the truth — the notary still isn't verifying it independently)
- **That the document is legally valid or enforceable** in every respect
- **That the signer fully understood every legal implication** of what they signed, beyond a general confirmation of understanding
- **That the transaction described in the document actually occurred** as stated

A notary's seal is not an endorsement of the document's substance — only of the identity verification and witnessing that took place.

## Why This Distinction Matters in Litigation

In a legal dispute, a notarized document carries real evidentiary value specifically around the question of *who signed it and whether they did so willingly* — which can be significant if someone later claims they never signed something, or that their signature was forged. The notarization creates documented, witnessed evidence addressing exactly that question.

What a notarized document doesn't automatically resolve is whether the underlying facts described in the document are true, or whether the agreement itself is fair, enforceable, or legally sound. Those are separate legal questions a court still has to evaluate.

## Sworn Affidavits: A Slightly Different Case

Because an affidavit specifically involves the signer swearing to the truth of its contents, a notarized affidavit does carry meaningfully more weight regarding the truthfulness of its statements than an ordinary notarized document — the swearing itself creates legal consequences (potentially including perjury) if the content turns out to be knowingly false. Our post on [what a sworn affidavit is](/blog/what-is-a-sworn-affidavit) covers this distinction in more detail.

## Can a Notarized Signature Still Be Challenged?

Yes — notarization makes a signature harder to successfully dispute, but it's not absolutely immune to challenge. If someone claims the notarization itself was improperly performed (identity wasn't actually verified, the signer wasn't actually present), that can become the subject of its own legal dispute, sometimes involving the notary's journal or, for RON, the session recording as supporting evidence.

## The Practical Takeaway

Notarization is a meaningful, legally significant step — but it's specifically about verifying identity and witnessing, not a blanket guarantee of a document's truth or validity. Understanding this scope helps set the right expectations for what notarizing a document actually accomplishes.
