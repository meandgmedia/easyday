"Notary public," "signing agent," "loan signing agent" — these terms circle around the same general idea but aren't perfectly interchangeable, and the differences matter more than people expect when you're trying to find the right person for a specific job.

## Notary Public: The Baseline Credential

A notary public is a state-commissioned official authorized to verify identity, witness signatures, and complete notarial acts. This is the foundational credential — every signing agent is a notary public, but the reverse isn't true.

## Signing Agent: A Specialized Role Built on Top

"Signing agent," in the context most people mean it, refers to a notary who has completed additional training specifically focused on handling real estate loan closing packages — understanding the document stack, where signatures and initials go, which documents need notarization, and how to keep the process moving efficiently for a title company or lender.

Importantly, this doesn't grant any *additional legal authority* beyond a standard notary commission — the notarial powers are identical. What's different is specialized knowledge and training relevant to a specific, document-heavy type of appointment.

## Why the Distinction Actually Matters for You

If you're a borrower with a straightforward document to notarize — an affidavit, a power of attorney — a general notary is entirely sufficient; no specialized signing agent training is needed for that kind of appointment. But if you're closing on a mortgage, working with someone specifically experienced in loan signing packages reduces the odds of a missed signature or notarization holding up your closing.

## Is "Signing Agent" a Regulated Term?

Not in the same formal sense that "notary public" is a defined legal commission. In practice, though, the term carries real professional meaning within the industry — title companies and signing services generally expect it to reflect actual training and experience with closing packages, not just a general notary willing to take on the work.

## A Quick Comparison
