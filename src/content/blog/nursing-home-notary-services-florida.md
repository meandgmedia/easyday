Getting older often means paperwork catches up with you — powers of attorney that should've been signed years ago, a trust that needs updating, a healthcare surrogate form nobody got around to. If a parent or loved one is in a nursing home or assisted living community, arranging a notary visit is usually simpler than families expect.

## Notaries Visit These Facilities Regularly

This isn't an unusual request. Mobile notaries visit nursing homes and assisted living communities often enough that most facilities have a general sense of how it works — sign in as a visitor, head to the resident's room or a common area, and complete the notarization there.

## Documents That Come Up Most Often

For residents in long-term care, a specific set of documents tends to come up again and again:

- **Durable power of attorney** — authorizing someone to manage finances
- **Healthcare surrogate designation** — naming a medical decision-maker
- **Living wills** — outlining end-of-life care preferences
- **Trust documents** — establishing or updating a living trust
- **Wills and codicils** — though these have their own separate execution requirements beyond notarization

If estate planning is the goal, it's worth coordinating with an elder law attorney beforehand to make sure the documents are drafted correctly — a notary's job is witnessing the signature, not drafting the document itself.

## The Question of Capacity

This is the part families sometimes worry about most, so let's address it directly. A notary's responsibility is to confirm that the person signing understands what they're signing and is doing so of their own free will. This doesn't mean someone needs to be sharp as a tack — plenty of people with mild memory issues or general cognitive decline can still clearly understand and consent to a specific document.

Where it gets more complicated is when there's genuine doubt about whether someone can understand the document at all. In those cases, a notary may need to decline the notarization, or ask for more context — sometimes from a physician — before proceeding. This isn't the notary being difficult; it's the exact safeguard notarization exists to provide.

## Coordinating With Facility Staff

While staff generally don't need to be part of the notarization itself, they can make the whole visit smoother:

- Confirming visiting hours and any current visitor restrictions
- Suggesting the best time of day based on the resident's routine
- Providing a private, quiet space for the signing if the resident's room isn't ideal

A quick call to the front desk before booking can save everyone a wasted trip.

## What Family Members Should Bring

- **The unsigned document(s)**
- **The resident's valid, government-issued photo ID**
- **Any required witnesses**, if the document needs them (a power of attorney needs two)
- **Contact information for facility staff**, in case the notary needs to check in upon arrival

## Timing Matters More Than People Realize

Estate and healthcare documents for aging family members have a way of becoming urgent right around the time they become difficult — a hospitalization, a decline in health, a sudden need for someone else to manage finances. If there's a document that's been sitting on a to-do list, it's worth getting done sooner rather than later, while the situation is calm rather than urgent.
