"Can I just use an online notary from anywhere?" is one of those questions that sounds like it should have a simple yes or no answer. It doesn't, quite — and the nuance is worth understanding before you assume your out-of-state situation will just work itself out.

## The Core Rule for Florida RON

Florida's Remote Online Notarization law generally requires that the **signer** be physically located within Florida at the time of the notarization, even though the interaction itself happens over video. The notary's commission is tied to Florida, and the legal authority to notarize is scoped to signers within the state, not to anyone anywhere with an internet connection.

This surprises people who assume "online" means "location doesn't matter." For RON specifically, location still matters — it's just that the location requirement is verified through the platform's technology (often IP address and device location checks) rather than by physically standing in the same room.

## What If You're a Florida Resident Traveling Out of State?

This is genuinely one of the more common edge cases. If you normally live in Florida but you're traveling when you need something notarized, whether a Florida RON notary can help depends on the exact terms of that notary's commission and the platform's location verification. Some situations allow it; many don't. If this applies to you, it's worth confirming directly before assuming a session will go through — the platform may simply block the session if it detects you're outside Florida.

## Using an Out-of-State Notary for a Florida Document

The reverse question comes up too: can someone in, say, Texas, use a Texas-commissioned online notary to notarize a document that's headed to Florida? Sometimes — it depends on what the receiving institution in Florida is willing to accept. Some documents and institutions accept notarization from any properly commissioned U.S. notary, regardless of state. Others specifically require a Florida notary, particularly for real estate transactions involving Florida property.

The safest approach, especially for anything tied to Florida real estate, courts, or state agencies: use a Florida-commissioned notary. It removes the guesswork entirely.

## Why Interstate RON Recognition Is Inconsistent

Notary law is fundamentally state-by-state, and Remote Online Notarization is a relatively recent addition to most states' legal frameworks — not all of them arrived at the same rules. Some states have adopted broader interstate recognition agreements for RON; others haven't gotten there yet. This patchwork is exactly why there's no clean, universal answer to "can I use any online notary from anywhere" — the honest answer depends on which two states (or one state and one country) are actually involved.

## A Practical Rule of Thumb

| Situation | Best Approach |
|---|---|
| You're in Florida, document is for Florida use | Florida RON notary — straightforward |
| You're a Florida resident traveling elsewhere | Confirm with the notary/platform before booking |
| Document is for use in another state | Check that state's and institution's requirements |
| Document is for use internationally | Confirm with the receiving country — may need an apostille |
