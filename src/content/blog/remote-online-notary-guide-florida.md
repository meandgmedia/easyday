## What Remote Online Notary Actually Means

A Remote Online Notary is a notary public who's been specifically commissioned by the state to perform notarizations using live audio-video technology instead of sitting across a table from you. Florida has allowed this since 2020, when the legislature updated Chapter 117 of the Florida Statutes to formally recognize online notarization as legally equivalent to the in-person kind.

That last part matters. A RON notarization isn't a workaround or a "close enough" substitute — it's the real thing, with the same legal standing as a wet-ink signature and a physical stamp. The session gets recorded, your identity gets verified through a mix of ID document scanning and knowledge-based verification questions, and you sign electronically using a secure platform built for exactly this purpose.

## How a RON Session Actually Works

Here's the play-by-play, since "secure video notarization" can sound more complicated than it is:

1. **You book a session.** Pick a time that works, upload your documents ahead of time so everything's ready to go.
2. **You verify your identity.** This usually involves scanning a government-issued ID and answering a handful of knowledge-based questions — think of it like the security questions your bank asks, but slightly more personal.
3. **You meet your notary on video.** We confirm who you are, review the document together, and make sure you understand what you're signing (a notary's job, always).
4. **You sign electronically.** Using a secure e-signature, right there on the platform.
5. **You get your notarized document.** Complete with an electronic notarial certificate and seal, ready to send wherever it needs to go.

That's genuinely the whole process. No printer, no drive, no waiting room chair with the wobbly leg.

## When RON Makes Sense (and When It Doesn't)

RON is a great fit for most everyday notarizations — affidavits, powers of attorney, business agreements, travel consent forms, and plenty more. It's especially useful when:

- You're short on time and can't fit in a drive across town
- You're notarizing something for someone in another part of Florida
- You need something handled outside typical business hours
- You just really, really don't want to leave the house

It's not always the right call, though. Some receiving institutions — certain courts, or specific out-of-state agencies — still want a physical wet signature. And if your document needs multiple signers in the same room for a particular reason, an in-person [mobile notary](/mobile-notary) visit might genuinely be simpler. When in doubt, a quick text settles it faster than guessing.
