Most notary appointments happen without anyone thinking twice about whether the notary is legitimately commissioned — and most of the time, that trust is entirely well-placed. But for high-stakes documents, or if something about a situation feels off, knowing how to actually verify a commission is a useful thing to have in your back pocket.

## Why This Occasionally Matters

A notary's authority comes directly from their state commission — without a valid, current commission, someone isn't legally authorized to notarize documents, regardless of how official they might seem. This becomes relevant in a few situations: verifying a notary you found online but haven't worked with before, confirming credentials for a particularly significant document, or simply being thorough when a lot is riding on getting the notarization right.

## How to Actually Check

Florida's Department of State maintains records of active notary commissions. A notary's commission number, along with their name, can typically be verified through the state's official notary records. If you're working with a notary and want to confirm their commission is active, it's entirely reasonable to ask for their commission number and verify it independently.

## What a Legitimate Notary's Seal Should Include

A proper Florida notary seal typically includes:

- The notary's name, exactly as commissioned
- The words "Notary Public – State of Florida"
- The notary's commission number
- The commission expiration date

If a seal is missing key information or looks noticeably informal or inconsistent, that's worth a closer look.

## Asking a Notary to Confirm Their Commission

If you want to verify before an appointment, simply asking is entirely appropriate: "Can you share your commission number?" A legitimate, professional notary will provide this without hesitation — it's public information anyway, since it appears on every document they notarize.

## Why an Expired Commission Is a Real Problem

If someone notarizes a document after their commission has expired, that notarization isn't legally valid — the person performing it, at that point, isn't actually authorized to act as a notary. This can create genuine complications for the document later, particularly for anything with legal significance like a deed or power of attorney. This is exactly why verification matters more for high-stakes documents than for something low-consequence.

## Red Flags Worth Noticing

- **Reluctance to provide a commission number** when asked
- **A seal missing standard information** like an expiration date
- **Pressure to skip standard identity verification steps**
- **Unusually low prices** that seem inconsistent with legitimate, licensed service

None of these guarantee a problem, but any of them are worth a second look before proceeding with something important.
