By the time you're sitting down for an actual trust signing appointment, the hard part — deciding how you want your assets managed and distributed — is usually already done with your estate planning attorney. What's left is making it official, and that process is more straightforward than the planning that led up to it.

## What a Trust Signing Actually Involves

A trust signing is the formal execution of a trust document — the moment it goes from a draft to a legally binding instrument. Depending on the type of trust and how it's structured, this typically involves:

- **Signing the trust agreement itself**
- **Signing any related documents**, such as a pour-over will or asset assignment documents
- **Witnessing and notarization**, following the same general formalities Florida requires for wills

## Who Needs to Be in the Room

- **The grantor** (the person creating the trust) — the primary signer
- **Co-trustees**, if the trust names more than one person managing it
- **Witnesses**, since Florida trust execution typically follows will-like formalities requiring witnesses
- **A notary**, to verify identities and complete the notarial certificate

If the trust involves multiple grantors — a married couple creating a joint trust, for example — both need to be present and sign.

## Why Trusts Follow Will-Like Formalities

Florida generally requires a trust to be executed with the same formalities as a will — signed, witnessed, and often notarized — because a trust is doing similar work to a will: directing how your assets are handled, in this case often while you're still alive and after. Treating the execution requirements seriously here isn't optional red tape; it's what makes the trust legally sound and harder to challenge later.

## A Typical Trust Signing Appointment

1. **Review the trust document** with your attorney beforehand, so the signing itself isn't the first time you're seeing it
2. **Sign the trust agreement**, witnessed as required
3. **Sign related documents** — pour-over wills, asset transfer paperwork, sometimes powers of attorney handled in the same sitting
4. **Notarization**, completing the legal formalities

Most appointments run 30 to 60 minutes, depending on how many related documents are being signed together.

## Funding the Trust: A Step That Comes After

Worth mentioning, since it trips people up: signing the trust document doesn't automatically move your assets into it. "Funding" the trust — retitling accounts, deeds, and other assets into the trust's name — is a separate step that happens afterward, often with guidance from your attorney or financial institution. A notary's role is limited to the signing itself, not the funding process.

## Can This Happen Remotely?

Often, yes. If your witnesses are comfortable joining a video call, a trust signing can be completed through [Remote Online Notary](/remote-online-notary), the same way a power of attorney can be. This is particularly useful if family members involved as witnesses or co-trustees live in different locations.
