If you've never had something notarized before, the whole process can feel like a mystery right up until you're actually sitting across from a notary. Here's exactly what to expect, so nothing catches you off guard.

## Before the Appointment

- **Have your document ready but unsigned.** Florida law requires you to sign in the notary's presence.
- **Have valid, government-issued photo ID on hand** — a driver's license, state ID, or passport.
- **Confirm any witnesses** are available, if your document requires them.

## Step 1: Identity Verification

The notary will ask to see your photo ID and compare it against you and your name on the document. This is a standard, quick step — not an interrogation, just a confirmation that you are who the document says you are.

## Step 2: A Brief Conversation About the Document

The notary will typically confirm that you understand generally what you're signing and that you're doing so willingly. This isn't a legal review of the document's content — it's a quick, straightforward check to make sure you're not confused or being pressured.

## Step 3: You Sign

With the notary watching, you sign the document. If it's a sworn document like an affidavit, you'll also verbally affirm the truth of the statement — usually a simple verbal confirmation, not an elaborate oath ceremony.

## Step 4: The Notary Completes the Certificate

The notary fills out the notarial certificate — a short statement confirming what type of notarization occurred (an acknowledgment or a jurat, depending on the document) — and applies their official seal.

## Step 5: You're Done

That's genuinely the whole process. Most single-document appointments wrap up in 15 to 20 minutes, start to finish.

## What's Different for Remote Online Notary

The core steps are identical — identity verification, brief review, signing, sealing — but conducted over live video instead of in person, with an added identity verification layer (an ID scan plus knowledge-based questions) before the session begins. ## What If Multiple People Need to Sign?

If your document requires multiple signers, each person goes through the same identity verification and signing process individually, though it typically happens within the same appointment if everyone's present together.

## What If You Have Questions Mid-Appointment?

Procedural questions — "where do I sign," "what does this stamp mean" — are exactly what a notary is there to answer. Questions about the document's legal meaning or whether you should be signing it at all are better addressed with an attorney before your appointment, since that falls outside what a notary is authorized to advise on.
